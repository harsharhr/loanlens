What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest is interest calculated on both the initial principal and the accumulated interest from previous periods. Often described as "interest on interest," compounding causes investments to grow at an accelerating rate over time.

Unlike simple interest — which is calculated only on the principal — compound interest grows exponentially, which is why Albert Einstein reportedly called it "the eighth wonder of the world." The longer the time horizon, the more dramatically compounding amplifies your returns.

How is Compound Interest Calculated?

The standard formula for compound interest is:

A = P ( 1 + r/n ) nt
AFinal amount (what you end up with) PPrincipal (your initial investment) rAnnual interest rate (as a decimal, e.g. 5% = 0.05) nNumber of times interest compounds per year tTime in years

Nominal vs. Effective Annual Rate

The nominal rate is the stated interest rate. The Effective Annual Rate (EAR) is the actual rate you earn after compounding kicks in, and it's always higher than the nominal rate (except when compounding annually).

Formula: EAR = (1 + r/n)n − 1

For example, 6% nominal with monthly compounding gives an EAR of approximately 6.17%. This difference becomes significant at higher rates and over longer periods.

The Rule of 72

A quick mental shortcut: divide 72 by your interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your investment. At 6%, money doubles in about 12 years (72 ÷ 6 = 12).

Frequently Asked Questions

How does the compound frequency affect my returns? +
The more frequently interest compounds, the higher your effective return. Daily compounding earns slightly more than monthly, which earns more than annually. The difference is small at low rates but grows with higher rates. For most savings accounts and fixed deposits in India, monthly or quarterly compounding is standard.
What is the difference between compound and simple interest? +
Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal: Interest = P × r × t. Compound interest includes interest earned in previous periods as part of the new principal. Over long periods, the difference is enormous — compound interest can produce dramatically higher returns even at the same nominal rate.
Can I use this calculator for SIP or recurring deposits? +
Yes. Use the "Deposits" contribution mode and set the deposit frequency to monthly. For SIP (Systematic Investment Plan), set the compound frequency to monthly and add your monthly SIP amount as a regular deposit. The calculator will show your projected corpus over the investment period.
What does "deposit timing" mean? +
"Beginning of period" means your regular deposit is added at the start of each compounding period — so it earns interest for that period. "End of period" means the deposit is added after interest is applied. Beginning-of-period compounding yields slightly higher returns because each deposit spends more time earning interest.
How is the effective annual rate calculated? +
EAR = (1 + r/n)^n − 1, where r is the nominal annual rate and n is the compounding frequency per year. For example, a 10% nominal rate compounded monthly gives EAR = (1 + 0.10/12)^12 − 1 ≈ 10.47%. This means you actually earn 10.47% on your money in real terms.
Is this calculator accurate for bank fixed deposits (FDs)? +
Yes. Set the compound frequency to "Quarterly" (the standard for most Indian banks) and enter the FD amount as your initial investment. For recurring deposits, use the monthly deposit mode. Note that tax deducted at source (TDS) on FD interest is not factored in — consult your bank for exact post-tax returns.
